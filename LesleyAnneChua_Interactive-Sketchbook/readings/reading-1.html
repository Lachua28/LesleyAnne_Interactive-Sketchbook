Reading Reflection 1

1. Affordances & Signifiers

In general, the main affordance of my micro-project is entertainment via being a short video game! I suppose the “LEVEL 1 of 10” text at the top right corner serves as a signifier that my project is a game to people who are familiar with that terminology. But in terms of perceived functions, my project affords stopping a ‘bus’ to feel rewarded. This is signified using instructional text, which is how a user would know what they can do if they encountered my project with no context. I think the affordance of the reward (which is a very minimal victory screen) is hidden since a user wouldn’t know they would get a victory screen once they finished all ten levels; unless they are motivated to on their own, they might not try to get through all ten.

2. Feedback

The feedback of my system is in the form of ‘paused’ screens whenever the bus stops moving, whether that be from the user clicking or the bus reaching the end of the screen/‘road’. In the latter situation, text shows up that states that the bus has missed the stops. If the user stops the bus themselves, they see text telling them whether they made it to the bus stop or not. A moment of unclear state communication is after you click to stop the bus: there is not an obvious differentiator between the fail and success screens between levels, other than text. You wouldn’t know you failed or succeeded unless you read the text, which is a waste of time compared to a red or green tint, for instance. I think Norman would criticize that you shouldn’t have to read so much in a game.

3. Mapping

I don’t think there is a relevant relationship between input and outcome of my game, since it is just “click” = “stop.” The intuitive part is that clicking is natural for. people on computers (which is the device that the majority of people who see my game would be on). Based on my peer’s feedback, I know that a confusion of the mapping is that you press a key to restart a level or continue to the next one, rather than simply clicking again. There are clear instructions for this, but it feels like an unnecessary new action. This was somewhat intentional; I originally did it on purpose to make my own testing easier, but I had intended to change it and just ran out of time to do so.

4. Constraints

I’m a little unsure since constraints weren’t covered in the provided section of the reading, but I guess the constraint is that you can only win a level by stopping the bus while it’s within a certain area of the screen. In my project, this area is visible as a green rectangle showing the exact area on the floor, as well as a sign shape to show the general area better. This guides behaviour in that it influences people to click in that area versus the rest of the road, which is grey.

5. The Biggest Design Weakness

Norman would probably say that the biggest design weakness in my project is the amount of words over intuitive visual cues. He mentions in chapter 1 that you shouldn’t need to read through a dense instruction manual to be able to use the basic functions of something. Of course, I don’t have as much text as an instruction manual, but the ideal instructions for my game might be the visual of a mouse click or even a shorter phrase. And, as I mentioned above, there is the slightly unintuitive difference of pressing a key to change levels, which Norman would probably agree could be a click instead.

6. What is Your Biggest Takeaway from the Text?

I like the reading! Funnily enough, my partner is an engineer and has the physical book, so he shares a lot of Norman’s viewpoints about engineers.

My biggest takeaway in relation to ideas in this class is that it agrees with what we previously discussed where design should influence behaviour. I found it interesting that the reading brought up this idea of interaction design being for such subconscious thoughts that people will even deny its existence/usefulness. I thought it was funny that Norman gives the example of engineers excusing their own errors as being something that shouldn’t be considered because they are errors, when really that is something that should be factored into designing functional things. This is amusing to me because my partner, as an engineer who pushes for logical answers, has definitely had that kind of debate with me before. I’ve learned from him that engineers will try to make things so that errors don’t happen, whereas designers know errors happen and should design to accommodate them.
